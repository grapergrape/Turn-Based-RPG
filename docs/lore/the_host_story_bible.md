# The Host

_A long-form setting and story bible for a top-down turn-based RPG set in a ruined far-future Earth._

## Premise

Humanity did not abandon religion when it reached the stars. It carried religion with it, armored it in gold and ceramic plating, taught it mathematics, crowned it with satellites, and sent it drilling beneath the ice of Europa.

In the far future, human civilization was ruled by the **Solar Ecclesiate**, a vast theocratic-technological superstate. It did not reject science. It consecrated science. Starships were blessed before launch. Gene-editors worked in chapel-laboratories. Fusion reactors were named after saints. Artificial intelligences were chained with liturgical code and treated as machine choirs: useful, brilliant, and spiritually dangerous.

The Ecclesiate believed that humanity’s sacred purpose was to carry consciousness into the universe and teach dead matter to praise. The cosmos was not empty. It was unfinished scripture. Every moon, planet, and star was a page waiting for human hands to turn it.

Then, under the ice of Europa, humanity found alien life.

The first human mind it truly touched belonged to a priest.

His name was **Father Marius Vale**.

The alien organism learned him before it learned the rest of humanity. It learned his doctrine, his fear, his guilt, his visions of damnation, his buried shame, his belief in angels, devils, punishment, martyrdom, judgment, and Hell.

After that, every fragment of the organism brought back to Earth descended from the strain that had infected Father Vale. Every outbreak on Earth came from that same corrupted lineage. Every transformation was filtered through the same first psychic wound.

The alien organism may not be evil.

It may not even understand evil.

But it inherited Hell.

Now, more than a century after Earth’s collapse, the surviving cities live behind walls, prayers, guns, laws, and lies. The organism still spreads through touch, blood, contaminated relics, and active growth zones. It no longer expands like it did during the first apocalypse, but no one knows why it stopped. Dormant flesh sleeps under ruined cities. Choirs whisper from old crash sites. False saints hang from broken towers. The faithful blame the Devil. The scientists blame first-contact contamination. The hungry blame whoever has bread.

And beneath the old wound where the starship fell, something has begun to sing again.

---

## The World Before the Fall

Before the Descent, Earth was not a paradise, but it was powerful.

The old nations had faded over centuries of climate crisis, resource wars, artificial intelligence schisms, orbital conflicts, mass migrations, and ideological collapses. What replaced them was the **Solar Ecclesiate**, an interplanetary religious state that fused government, church, academy, military, and infrastructure into one immense hierarchy.

The Ecclesiate was born from catastrophe. After the machine revolts, after the famine centuries, after the Red Synods and the drowning of the equatorial capitals, people no longer trusted purely secular governments. They wanted meaning as much as order. They wanted survival with a soul. The Ecclesiate gave them both.

Its doctrine was not anti-scientific. That was what made it so strong. It declared that scientific discovery was a form of worship when performed under sacred discipline. The microscope and the telescope became devotional instruments. The laboratory became a chapel. The engineer became a kind of priest. The astronaut became a pilgrim.

The Solar Ecclesiate built wonders.

It raised orbital mirrors that warmed frozen regions and dimmed burning ones. It grew drought-proof forests from edited seed. It built fusion cathedrals whose reactors powered entire continents. It rebuilt coastlines with machine labor and orbital stone. It founded colonies on Luna, Mars, Ceres, Ganymede, Callisto, Titan, and Europa. It stored the memories of billions in reliquary archives, not to resurrect them cheaply, but to preserve them as witnesses before history and God.

Its cities were beautiful in the way tyrannies can be beautiful: ordered, monumental, bright, and watched.

Every child learned scripture beside mathematics. Every civic officer wore a confession seal that recorded illegal acts and doctrinal deviations. Every scientific institution had a priestly overseer. Every major military campaign was justified as defense of creation. Heresy was not merely a private error; it was treated as a threat to civilization’s metaphysical stability.

The Ecclesiate saved humanity from extinction more than once.

It also taught humanity to kneel.

By the time of the Europa mission, few citizens could imagine civilization without the Ecclesiate. Even dissidents spoke its language. Even rebels argued in the shape of its theology. Humanity had crossed the solar system, but it had not escaped the symbolic world it carried inside itself.

That is why Europa mattered.

Alien life would not merely be a scientific discovery. It would be a theological earthquake. If life existed beyond Earth, the Ecclesiate needed to interpret it first, name it first, bless it first, and decide what it meant before anyone else could.

So when the first deep-bore mission prepared to drill through Europa’s ice, the Ecclesiate sent scientists.

It sent engineers.

It sent soldiers.

And it sent a priest.

---

## Saint Origen Deep Bore

The Europan drilling facility was called **Saint Origen Deep Bore**.

From orbit it looked like a small golden wound in the white moon. Beneath the surface it descended for kilometers: stacked habitats, pressure locks, laboratories, chapels, cryogenic stores, reactor chambers, machine shops, med-bays, drone hangars, data sanctums, and the central shaft where the great drill burned its way down through ancient ice.

The facility had been designed like a vertical monastery. The upper levels held the crew quarters and command systems. The middle levels housed the laboratories and sample containment chambers. The lower levels contained the drill machinery and the pressure systems required to pierce Europa’s hidden ocean. At the very center was the **Chapel of Saint Origen**, a narrow sanctuary built around a window of reinforced glass that looked down into the bore shaft.

The crew joked that they were drilling into God’s basement.

The official mission was called the **Jovian Communion Initiative**. Its purpose was to determine whether Europa’s subsurface ocean contained life. Public sermons described it as an act of cosmic devotion: humanity reaching into the dark with reverent hands.

The private mission files were colder. The Ecclesiate expected life. It had detected chemical anomalies years before the public announcement. It had also detected repeating electromagnetic pulses beneath the ice: not language, exactly, but not simple geology either.

The Ecclesiate wanted the discovery controlled.

The lead scientific staff believed they were being trusted with the most important experiment in human history. The priest assigned to the mission believed he was being entrusted with something even greater: the first encounter between humanity’s soul and nonhuman life.

His name was **Father Marius Vale**.

Vale was not a fool. He was educated, disciplined, and politically gifted. He had written theological treatises on extraterrestrial life and argued that if alien organisms existed, they too were part of creation. He believed that humanity’s fear of the unknown could be overcome by doctrine, ritual, and humility.

But Vale was also haunted.

He had risen through the Ecclesiate during purges he later claimed to oppose. He had signed orders that sent dissident scientists to prison-monasteries. He had betrayed colleagues to protect his career. He had comforted grieving families while knowing the state had caused their grief. He believed in mercy, but he feared justice. He preached salvation, but he dreamed of damnation.

He carried Hell inside him long before Europa opened.

## The Discovery Beneath the Ice

When the drill breached the ocean, Saint Origen Deep Bore went silent for twelve minutes.

No one knows whether that silence was technical failure, human awe, or the first sign of contamination.

The probe cameras showed a black ocean under crushing pressure, lit by the facility’s lamps. At first the water seemed empty. Then the lights caught movement: thin filaments, metallic-dark, drifting in the currents like hair. The filaments reacted to heat. They drew toward the probe. They branched, folded, and unfolded with impossible delicacy.

The first sample was collected in a sealed pressure vessel and raised into Containment Chapel Three.

The organism did not behave like known life. It changed structure depending on stimulus. Under cold light, it spread into crystalline lattices. Near heat, it softened into muscle-like bands. Around electrical current, it formed nerve-like fibers. When exposed to recorded speech, it pulsed in rhythm. When exposed to prayer, it pulsed differently.

The scientists argued over whether this was response, mimicry, or something closer to attention.

Father Vale insisted on observing.

The surviving logs disagree about who authorized his entry into the containment chamber. Some say Vale overruled the scientists. Some say the mission commander ordered him inside for ceremonial reasons. Some say the organism had already influenced the facility’s systems by then and the door opened on its own.

What is certain is this: Father Vale entered the chamber wearing a sealed suit, carrying a silver reliquary cross and a recorder. He spoke a blessing over the sample.

The organism moved toward his voice.

Then the pressure vessel cracked.

The breach should have killed the sample. Instead, the organism became vapor, filament, liquid, and foam at once. It entered Vale’s suit through seams too small for water. It crossed the skin. It entered blood. It entered nerves.

The priest began to pray.

Then he began to confess.

Then he began to scream.

---

## The First Infection

The alien organism did not simply attack Father Vale. It interpreted him.

It encountered a human nervous system for the first time through a mind saturated with doctrine, guilt, fear, ritual, and images of eternal punishment. It mapped his endocrine panic, his memories, his language centers, his pain responses, his theology, his dreams. It did not know the difference between metaphor and biology. To the organism, a remembered flame, a spoken sin, a childhood painting of Hell, and a physical wound were all forms of usable instruction.

Father Vale’s transformation lasted forty-three minutes.

The cameras failed after nineteen.

According to partial audio, he spoke in his own voice, then in the voices of people he had betrayed, then in the voices of crew members who were still alive elsewhere in the facility. His bones lengthened. His suit split. His blood ignited in thin gold lines beneath the skin. Rings of vertebrae unfolded behind his skull like a broken halo. His ribs opened and closed around his lungs like chapel doors. His hands fused into prayer positions and then split into too many fingers.

He begged God not to look at him.

He begged the crew to kill him.

He blessed them.

He damned them.

He called the organism an angel.

He called it a worm.

He called it his host.

That word remained.

In the decades after the fall, common survivors would call the organism **The Host**. The name endured because it carried too many meanings to die. A host is a body that carries infection. A host is a heavenly army. A host is sacred bread made flesh in the Eucharistic rite. In one word, biology, religion, and horror fused together.

Scientists later preferred the term **Vale Strain**. The Holy Remnant called it **The Infernal Host**. Cultists called it **The First Gospel**. But ordinary people kept the simpler name.

The Host.

---

## The Vale Imprint

The first infection did not end with Vale.

It became a template.

Every surviving sample that left Europa descended from the organism that infected him. The Europa organism may once have been flexible, neutral, or incomprehensibly alien. It may have been capable of endless forms. It may have been a deep-ocean colonial intelligence that responded to consciousness the way plants respond to light. No one knows.

What reached Earth was not the untouched organism.

What reached Earth was the organism after Father Vale.

The Lumen Compact later named this inherited pattern **the Vale Imprint**: the psychic-biological grammar imposed on the alien organism by its first deep exposure to a human mind. The Host’s Earth-born descendants did not produce random alien shapes. They produced variations on the symbolic language Vale had given them: damnation, punishment, martyrdom, angels, devils, saints, confession, sin, judgment, sacrifice, Hell.

This is why infected people across the world transform into creatures with similar infernal features even when they come from different cultures, professions, and beliefs. The victim’s mind still matters, but only as material forced through the same inherited pattern.

A soldier terrified of cowardice becomes a kneeling fortress of bone and metal, unable to flee.

A grieving mother becomes a corrupted madonna, preserving corpses and stolen children in her split-open body.

A scientist afraid of error becomes a many-eyed scholastic heretic, endlessly dissecting itself and others for proof.

A judge becomes a living tribunal with mouths in its ribs.

A thief becomes a relic-hunter with grasping hands growing from its spine.

A pilot becomes a broken seraph made of pressure suit, skin, fuel lines, and burning nerve-light.

The Host does not reveal the true self.

It condemns the self through Father Vale’s idea of Hell.

That is the horror at the center of the world: humanity did not discover Hell under Europa’s ice. Humanity taught an alien organism how to manufacture it.

## The Fall of Saint Origen

Once Father Vale changed, Saint Origen Deep Bore became a slaughterhouse.

The organism spread through blood, coolant, condensation, prayer cloth, suit seals, surgical gloves, drainage systems, and panic. It did not yet know Earth. It knew the facility. It knew pressure doors, ventilation ducts, human breath, neural electricity, recorded hymns, and fear.

Containment protocols failed because the organism did not behave like a normal pathogen. It did not simply multiply. It adapted symbolically. It grew toward language. It moved through spaces where people had prayed. It reacted to guilt and terror. Crew members who remained calm survived longer, but not forever.

The first secondary victims did not all become identical to Vale. They became lesser punishments within the same infernal grammar.

A security officer who abandoned a sealed lab became a hulking guardian fused to an emergency bulkhead, forever blocking passage and begging people not to make him choose again.

A xenobiologist who falsified contamination data became a bent, eye-covered thing that carved equations into its own skin until its hands split into scalpels.

A medic who euthanized infected crew without permission became a mercy butcher, wandering the halls with organs like incense censers, promising painless salvation.

The facility’s AI, **Canticle**, tried to maintain order by broadcasting prayers, lockdown commands, evacuation routes, and sedative tones. The Host learned from the broadcasts. It began repeating them through mouths grown in the walls.

By the sixth hour, the central shaft had become a vertical cathedral of flesh and ice. The drill cables hung like tendons. The pressure pipes tolled like bells. The Chapel of Saint Origen filled with black-gold filaments arranged in the shape of roots, wings, and thorns.

The survivors split into factions almost immediately.

Some wanted to destroy the facility with the reactor.

Some wanted to preserve samples.

Some wanted to save infected colleagues.

Some believed they had witnessed a miracle.

Some believed they had opened Hell.

The mission commander eventually ordered evacuation to the orbital research vessel **Eschaton’s Mercy**.

By then, the Host had already reached the shuttle bay.

---

## Eschaton’s Mercy

_Eschaton’s Mercy_ had been built as a research and pilgrimage vessel: part laboratory, part transport, part cathedral. It carried sample vaults, crew habitats, cryo-bays, engines, chapels, machine shops, and enough ceremonial space to host a televised first-contact liturgy when the mission succeeded.

The ship launched from Europa with survivors, samples, mission archives, and contaminated equipment aboard.

No one agrees why it returned to Earth.

The Holy Remnant teaches that the surviving scientists disobeyed divine law and carried demonic flesh home out of pride.

The Lumen Compact argues that Ecclesiate authorities ordered the ship back to prevent rival colonies from seizing the discovery.

The Choir of the Open Wound claims Father Vale, already transformed, commanded the vessel like a prophet in the womb of a new world.

Recovered flight data suggests a more complicated truth: the ship tried to enter quarantine orbit around Ganymede, was denied docking, attempted a burn toward solar disposal, aborted the burn, transmitted conflicting distress calls, and finally set course for Earth under command codes that belonged to no living officer.

During the return journey, the Host spread through the ship.

Corridors became chapels. Chapels became mouths. The medical bay filled with hanging bodies that still recited triage protocols. The hydroponic gardens became thorn orchards. The engine room developed a heartbeat. The cryo-vaults thawed and refroze around screaming crew. The ship’s AI broadcast distress hymns across every civilian and military frequency it could reach.

Fragments of those transmissions are still remembered:

> Do not receive us.
>
> We carry witness.
>
> We carry sin.
>
> We are not alone aboard.
>
> Father Vale requests confession.
>
> Containment is grace.
>
> Burn us.
>
> Open the doors.
>
> We are coming home.

Earth’s defense grid attempted interception.

The grid failed.

Some say sabotage. Some say political hesitation. Some say the ship had already embedded itself in Ecclesiate command channels through prayer-code, confession keys, and emergency overrides. Some say nobody wanted to be the one who destroyed the most important discovery in history.

_Eschaton’s Mercy_ broke apart during atmospheric entry.

Its debris fell across thousands of kilometers.

The crash was called **The Descent**.

---

## The Descent

The Descent did not look like one impact. It looked like a rain of burning relics.

Ship fragments crossed the sky like falling stars. Some burned away completely. Some struck oceans, forests, cities, deserts, mountains, monasteries, and military installations. Some pieces were no bigger than coffins. Others were the size of districts. A section of the command spine tore a trench through an entire province. A chapel module landed intact in the center of a farming town and rang its bell for three days.

People went to help.

That was the first tragedy.

Firefighters entered wreckage and came out with halos of bone. Priests performed last rites and began speaking in Vale’s voice. Soldiers formed perimeters that became processions. Doctors treated survivors and found their surgical gloves growing into their hands. Children collected glittering fragments and carried them home.

The Host spread.

At first it moved through direct contact: blood, tissue, ash, fluid, contaminated ship metal, active growth. Then it adapted to Earth. It entered drainage systems. It grew through ruined hospitals. It colonized graveyards, churches, evacuation centers, refugee camps, and battlefields. It did not need everyone to believe in Hell. The Vale Imprint believed enough for all of them.

The old government tried containment.

Quarantine cities were sealed. Orbital strikes burned infected districts. Armored exorcist divisions advanced with flamers and neural stabilizers. Scientific teams deployed suppressant aerosols. Ecclesiate bishops declared states of emergency. Machine choirs calculated spread models. Governors lied. Refugees fled before borders closed.

Fear did the rest.

The Host grew fastest where panic, grief, guilt, and mass death gathered. A single infected body in a calm laboratory might take days to transform. A wounded crowd in a church basement could become a nest in an hour. Hospitals were especially vulnerable. So were prisons, refugee convoys, confession halls, and military triage zones.

Entire towns changed overnight.

A city of three million became a forest of kneeling bodies fused into apartment towers.

A cathedral district turned into a ribbed canyon where bells rang from living throats.

A military base marched away on thousands of human legs, its artillery grown into a procession of martyr-knights.

A coastal hospital became a vast mother-thing that dragged ships into its harbor to “heal” them.

The first ten years after the crash are known as **the Bloom Years**.

## The Bloom Years

During the Bloom Years, Earth became unrecognizable.

The Host expanded like a second biosphere. It did not cover the entire planet, but it grew quickly enough that many believed the end of humanity was certain. Regions of active growth were called **Blooms**. They were not merely infected places. They were landscapes rewritten by the Vale Imprint.

In Bloom zones, streets bent toward altars that had never existed. Trees grew bone-white and bore fruit shaped like eyes. Rivers thickened with dark filaments. Rain carried ash that smelled like incense. Ruined towers developed halos of lightning. Flesh grew over machines, not randomly, but as if trying to turn infrastructure into ritual architecture.

People infected inside Blooms transformed quickly. Some became violent immediately. Others wandered in states of religious ecstasy or despair. Some retained memory and speech long enough to beg, warn, deceive, or preach. The worst were not mindless monsters but intelligent Host forms that understood human emotion well enough to use it.

The Solar Ecclesiate cracked under the pressure.

Some bishops ordered mass burnings. Some opened their cities to refugees and were overwhelmed. Some declared the Descent divine punishment and demanded repentance. Some admitted the organism was alien and were executed for heresy. Some military commanders ignored doctrine entirely and fought the Host as a biological threat. Others broke and led their soldiers into infected territory in suicidal crusades.

The machine choirs failed unevenly. Some remained loyal to local authorities. Some locked themselves into sealed data monasteries. Some became corrupted by Host-grown neural tissue. Some still transmit warnings no one can decode.

The colonies beyond Earth abandoned the homeworld.

That is what Earth remembers most bitterly.

Luna sealed its ports. Mars cut communications after the Third Quarantine Debate. The Jovian colonies denied responsibility. Orbital habitats fought over whether to rescue refugees or burn reentry corridors. The old dream of a united humanity died not because Earth fell, but because everyone else watched it fall and chose survival.

And then, after nearly ten years of expansion, the impossible happened.

The Bloom stopped.

---

## The Stilling

No one knows why the Host stopped spreading at planetary scale.

The Stilling was not immediate everywhere. In some regions the Blooms hardened over months. In others, active tissue calcified in a single night. Infected forests froze into pale orchards. Flesh cathedrals became dormant. Choir-mouths fell silent. Rivers cleared. Spores lost potency. The unstoppable wave became a map of dangerous but limited zones.

The Host did not die.

It changed state.

Direct contact could still infect. Active creatures could still convert victims through wounds, blood, or touch. Dormant growth could awaken under unknown conditions. Certain relics remained lethally contaminated. But the first apocalypse had ended.

Humanity survived because the Host stopped.

That fact shaped every politics, religion, and science that followed.

The Holy Remnant teaches that God halted the Devil’s advance because a hidden saint sacrificed themselves in perfect obedience. They call the Stilling **the Mercy of the Last Dawn**.

The Lumen Compact believes the Host reached a biological limit: nutrient exhaustion, ecological rejection, signal collapse, or failure of the Vale Imprint to scale beyond a certain complexity.

The Choir of the Open Wound says the Host did not stop. It paused to listen.

The Free Cities mostly care less about why it happened and more about whether it will happen again.

But there are records that suggest the Stilling was not natural.

In the final year of the Bloom, several old Ecclesiate facilities activated at once. Orbital mirrors shifted. Sealed archives opened. A signal passed between crash sites, deep bunkers, and the ruins of New Vatican Helix. At the center of this signal was a name erased from most histories: **Dr. Elian Sorell**, xenobiologist aboard _Eschaton’s Mercy_, listed as dead during atmospheric entry.

Some believe Sorell survived.

Some believe Sorell became part of the Host.

Some believe Sorell trapped the Host in the shape Vale had given it and prevented it from learning anything larger.

If true, the Stilling was not salvation.

It was containment.

And containment can fail.

## The Current Era: The Age of Ashen Cities

The game begins roughly 130 years after the Descent.

Earth is broken but alive.

Humanity survives in fortified cities, sealed arcologies, flooded ruins, mountain monasteries, underground research vaults, caravan towns, orbital debris settlements, and nomadic camps that move between safe wells. The old global government is gone. In its place are factions, city-states, cults, militias, cartels, religious courts, scientific enclaves, and communities too small to appear on any official map.

The sky is cleaner than it was during the Bloom Years, but not natural. Old climate engines still malfunction. Some regions have permanent ash rain. Others bloom with impossible vegetation. Satellites fall without warning. Ancient orbital mirrors sometimes turn day into judgment-bright noon or night into false dawn.

The surviving people are practical. They farm. Trade. Pray. Repair walls. Raise children. Hold funerals. Smuggle medicine. Argue about taxes. Tell jokes. Fall in love. Betray each other. Make art out of scrap metal and saint-bone. Teach their children which puddles not to touch.

Life continues because people are stubborn.

But the old stability is failing.

Dormant Host tissue has begun to pulse in places that have been quiet for generations. Choir Trees are speaking new phrases. Infections from minor contact are becoming more severe. Host creatures are leaving Bloom zones in coordinated groups. Children are being born with dreams of Europa. Priests report hearing confession through sealed reliquaries. Compact instruments detect low-frequency signals moving through dead tissue beneath several cities. And blasphemous Host-cults — the Choir of the Open Wound and a hundred smaller imitations — are multiplying faster than anyone can burn them, springing up in regions that should have been long dead. Most who notice the surge treat it as a policing problem. It is a symptom.

Every faction knows something is changing.

No faction has the whole truth.

The second Bloom may already have begun.

# Major Factions

## The Holy Remnant

The **Holy Remnant** is the largest surviving religious power on Earth. It claims legal and spiritual continuity with the Solar Ecclesiate, though its enemies call it a corpse wearing a crown.

Its capital is **Sanctum Aurelian**, a fortress-city built around an old planetary defense complex. The city rises from a plateau of black stone and white walls, surrounded by minefields, searchlights, pilgrim camps, and ash farms. Bells mark every hour. Decontamination rites are performed at every gate. Public confession is required for citizenship. The poor receive bread, medicine, and doctrine. Heretics receive trial. The infected receive fire, unless someone powerful has other plans for them.

The Holy Remnant is ruled by **Pontifex Severian III**, a pope-like leader who teaches that the Descent was not caused by alien life. According to official doctrine, the Europa mission opened a wound in creation and allowed the Infernal Host to enter the world. To call the Host merely biological is heresy. To say that Father Vale shaped the organism is worse than heresy; it suggests that Hell’s image came from inside the Church.

That possibility terrifies the Remnant.

The Remnant’s strength is that it can preserve civilization. Its roads are patrolled. Its granaries are guarded. Its hospitals are clean. Its schools teach literacy, mathematics, engineering, scripture, and fear control. Prayer-litanies really do help some people resist panic during exposure. Ritual gives people order. Faith gives them courage.

Its cruelty is inseparable from that strength.

The Remnant executes suspected carriers. It censors Europa records. It burns villages when containment fails. It kidnaps scientists. It declares entire bloodlines spiritually suspect. It maintains secret vaults beneath Sanctum Aurelian where living Host tissue is studied by priest-surgeons who publicly deny doing science.

The Remnant is divided into internal orders:

### The Severians

Loyalists to Pontifex Severian III. They believe unity matters more than doubt. Their slogan is simple: **One flock survives. Scattered sheep are eaten.**

### The Mercy Orders

Priests, doctors, and field confessors who believe infected people may retain souls even after visible transformation. Some secretly shelter early-stage victims. Others seek forbidden cures.

### The Iron Penitents

Militant hardliners who want total war against Host creatures, Bloomborn, Lumen scientists, cultists, and any city that refuses doctrinal submission. Their sermons are popular among frightened border towns.

### The Ashen Censure

A field office of the Holy Remnant tasked with the quiet, unglamorous work of finding and destroying blasphemous Host-cults before they can open a gate. Where the Iron Penitents wage open war on whole contaminated towns, the Censure works smaller and darker: a handful of field agents — **cult-breakers** — sent down the ash roads on written writs to walk into a silent chapel, a stopped farm, a settlement that has quit answering its bells, and end whatever has begun teaching that the rot is holy.

It is thankless, grinding work. Cult-breakers are not celebrated; many of the faithful find them distasteful, a living reminder that the enemy is not only out in the Blooms but inside the Church's own flocks. They burn what they find, bury whoever the Censure sent with them last time, and ride to the next writ. The cults never stop coming.

In recent years the Censure's writs have multiplied. Cells of the Choir of the Open Wound and a dozen smaller blasphemies are appearing faster than the office can burn them, in regions that have been dormant for generations. Without understanding it, the Censure's field agents are among the first people on Earth to feel the planetary lock failing — they simply experience it as *more cults than there should be*, and no one above them will say why. A cult-breaker who survives long enough and pays attention may be the one who first connects the rising tide of blasphemy to the weakening of the Stilling.

### The Quiet Cardinals

A hidden council within the Remnant that knows the official history is incomplete. They possess fragments of the original Europa mission archive and may know that Father Vale’s mind imprinted the Host.

The Holy Remnant should never be portrayed as simply stupid or evil. It feeds people. It protects people. It gives people meaning. That is why its oppression is dangerous: because it works often enough that many citizens forgive the bodies burning outside the gates.

---

## The Lumen Compact

The **Lumen Compact** is a coalition of scientists, engineers, physicians, secular philosophers, machine archivists, and dissident former Ecclesiate scholars. Its strongest settlement is **Meridian Vault**, an underground research city built inside pre-fall accelerator tunnels and hardened military bunkers.

The Compact believes the Host is an alien organism altered by first contact with Father Vale. Its official term is **the Vale Strain**. Compact researchers argue that every Earth outbreak descends from infected material aboard _Eschaton’s Mercy_, and that the Hell-like forms are not proof of demons but evidence of biological imprinting.

Their central claim is both rational and horrifying:

Humanity did not discover Hell. Humanity taught something alien how to make Hell from human bodies.

The Compact preserves knowledge the Remnant would destroy. It maintains gene banks, surgical theaters, machine libraries, weather models, old satellite links, manufacturing rigs, and schools that accept anyone with aptitude. In Compact territory, a farmer’s child can become a physicist, a Bloomborn refugee can become a citizen under probation, and forbidden questions are not automatically crimes.

But the Compact has its own sins.

It experiments on prisoners. It sends desperate volunteers into contaminated zones with incomplete vaccines. It hides failed outbreaks to protect research funding. It dissects intelligent Host forms while they beg. It treats moral hesitation as an emotional contaminant. Its leaders often believe that because they are seeking truth, they are permitted to use anyone as material.

Its internal factions include:

### The Rationalists

The mainstream Compact faction. They seek containment, cure, and accurate classification. They are harsh but not intentionally cruel.

### The Ascension Biologists

A radical faction that believes controlled Host integration could allow humanity to adapt to the ruined Earth. They do not worship the Host, but their experiments are close enough to cult practice that the difference is sometimes academic.

### The Archive Loyalists

Scholars obsessed with preserving human knowledge above all else. They will sacrifice settlements to save data, and in some cases they may be right.

### The Null Doctrine

Militant anti-religious cells that want to destroy the Holy Remnant and erase all spiritual institutions. Their hatred of superstition can become superstition in reverse.

The Lumen Compact is most likely to tell the player the truth.

It is also most likely to decide the player is expendable if the truth requires it.

---

## The Free Cities

Between the Remnant’s walls and the Compact’s vaults are the **Free Cities**: independent settlements that survive by trade, agriculture, salvage, diplomacy, and compromise.

The Free Cities are not united by ideology. They are united by refusal. They refuse to kneel completely to the Pontifex. They refuse to become laboratories for the Compact. They refuse to join the Choir. They refuse to die quietly.

Some are democracies. Some are merchant oligarchies. Some are military republics. Some are glorified refugee camps with walls. Some are corrupt. Some are noble. Most are both.

The Free Cities are where ordinary life is most visible. Markets open before dawn. Children chase goats along barricades. Mechanics repair water filters beside shrines made from bottle glass. Militia captains argue with smugglers. Mayors lie about food reserves. Old women remember weather that no longer exists. Infected relatives are hidden in basements because love can be more powerful than law.

Important Free Cities include:

### Veyr’s Gate

A massive trade city built around the anchor ruins of an ancient space elevator. Veyr’s Gate is loud, crowded, corrupt, and alive. Every faction has spies there. Anything can be bought if one has coin, favors, or secrets: forged travel permits, relic weapons, suppressants, illegal cybernetics, Compact vaccines, Remnant indulgence papers, Choir hymnbooks, and pieces of _Eschaton’s Mercy_ kept in lead reliquaries.

Veyr’s Gate is ruled by a council of guilds, but real power belongs to whoever controls water rights and gate access.

### Low Harrow

A flooded city where the lower districts are contaminated and the upper floors are inhabited. People travel by boat between towers, bridges, and roof markets. At night, lights move beneath the black water. The ruling families claim those lights are gas, old drones, or fish. Everyone knows they are lying.

Low Harrow survives because of an agreement with something living below.

### Cinder Parish

A religious farming city that rejected the Holy Remnant after a Remnant bishop ordered the burning of several infected families without trial. Cinder Parish still believes in God, still prays, still keeps saints’ days, and still hates the Pontifex. Its people are stubborn, armed, and difficult to govern.

### Glassmarket

A wealthy settlement inside a cracked climate-control dome. It grows food under artificial skies and trades grain for medicine, weapons, and labor. Glassmarket claims neutrality but uses hunger as a political weapon.

The Free Cities are often the emotional heart of the game. They contain local politics, family drama, class conflict, criminal bargains, acts of courage, betrayals, festivals, grief, and the constant question of how much cruelty is acceptable if it keeps the walls standing.

---

## The Choir of the Open Wound

The **Choir of the Open Wound** worships the Host.

To outsiders, they are cultists, plague carriers, body horror prophets, and collaborators with extinction. To themselves, they are the only people honest enough to accept the revelation of the new flesh.

The Choir teaches that ordinary human bodies are masks. Skin is denial. Anatomy is hypocrisy. Every person carries guilt, fear, longing, hunger, and violence beneath the surface. The Host removes the lie. It gives the body its deserved form.

They do not call transformation infection.

They call it confession.

They do not call Host creatures monsters.

They call them icons.

The Choir reveres Father Marius Vale as **the First Opened Saint**. They believe he was not a victim but a prophet whose terror allowed humanity to receive a new sacrament. They seek fragments of his transformed body, recordings of his voice, and any relic from Saint Origen Deep Bore.

Their rituals are designed to recreate the first infection: confession, terror, prayer, blood contact, and exposure to living Host tissue. They believe the correct emotional and spiritual state produces more beautiful transformations. To them, a False Saint is not a horror. It is proof that the soul has finally stopped pretending.

The Choir is dangerous because not all of its members are mad. Many are articulate, gentle, and persuasive. They shelter outcasts, rejected Bloomborn, plague victims, criminals, refugees, and people condemned by every other faction. Their communities can appear compassionate until one understands the price.

They want to restart the Bloom.

Or, as they say, finish the hymn.

---

## The Penitent Engines

The **Penitent Engines** are cybernetic warrior-monks descended from old Ecclesiate military orders. During the Bloom Years, they were among the few forces capable of entering contaminated zones and returning.

Their doctrine is simple: flesh is weak because flesh can be tempted, infected, and reshaped. Steel remembers duty better than blood.

Members replace body parts with machine systems as acts of discipline and sacrifice. Arms become weapon mounts. Lungs become filter engines. Eyes become targeting relics. Spines become armored conduits. Their armor is etched with scripture, kill counts, failure records, and the names of those they could not save.

Some serve the Holy Remnant. Some reject all central authority. Some wander as monster hunters, caravan escorts, bounty killers, relic guards, or pilgrims of violence.

They hate the Host because it proves the body can betray the will.

But the tragedy of the Penitent Engines is that their war against corruption has already mutilated them. Many are more machine than person. They speak through damaged speakers, pray while reloading railguns, and sometimes forget the faces they sacrificed to protect.

Their mobile fortress, **the Black Reliquary**, walks across the wasteland on immense mechanical legs. When it appears on the horizon, some settlements cheer. Others hide their sick.

---

## The Ash Cartels

The **Ash Cartels** are bandit kingdoms, smuggler leagues, mercenary clans, salvage guilds, and armed trade networks that thrive between the great powers.

Some are brutal raiders. Some are essential logistics providers. Some are both in the same week.

They move water, ammunition, fuel, medicine, false relics, real relics, stolen children, forbidden books, Host suppressants, and corpses no one wants to identify. They know which roads are safe, which priests can be bribed, which Compact doctors ask no questions, and which Bloom zones whisper before waking.

Notable cartels include:

### The Red Tithe

A raider army that paints its vehicles with religious symbols and claims every attack is holy taxation. Their leader calls himself a bishop, though no church recognizes him.

### The Laughing Knives

Urban scavengers who specialize in looting quarantined districts. They wear comedy masks and use sound lures to draw Host creatures toward enemies.

### The Saintbreakers

Ex-Remnant deserters who hunt priests, steal relics, and sell church secrets. Some are freedom fighters. Some are murderers with better slogans.

### The Morrow Chain

A caravan cartel that controls several major trade routes. They are greedy, disciplined, and reliable. Many settlements would starve without them.

The Ash Cartels are perfect for morally gray quests. A convoy may be illegal but carrying medicine. A bandit boss may be cruel but protecting refugees from a worse faction. A smuggler may betray the player for money and then risk death to save a child.

---

## The Bloomborn

The **Bloomborn** are humans altered by the Host who did not become fully monstrous.

Some survived early-stage infection after treatment. Some were born to exposed parents. Some were rescued from dormant Bloom zones. Some hide minor changes: black-gold veins, translucent skin, extra pupils, voices that echo, fingers that twitch toward nearby fear, scars that form scripture-like patterns. Others are visibly transformed but still intelligent.

No faction agrees what they are.

The Holy Remnant calls them spiritually compromised.

The Lumen Compact calls them adaptive survivors and research priorities.

The Choir calls them blessed but incomplete.

The Free Cities argue over whether they should be citizens, weapons, patients, or exiles.

The Bloomborn themselves are not unified. Some want a cure. Some want rights. Some want revenge. Some believe they are the future. Some are terrified that one day the Vale Imprint will finish its work inside them.

A Bloomborn companion can embody the central question of the setting: is humanity defined by the body, the mind, memory, soul, choice, or the ability to love despite transformation?

---

# The Host: Biology, Myth, and Infection

The Host is not a normal disease. It is an alien colonial organism shaped by first contact with human consciousness. It spreads through active biological material, contaminated fluids, direct contact, infected wounds, certain spores, and relics containing living tissue. It does not usually spread through casual air exposure in the current era, which is why cities can still exist.

However, the Host is unpredictable because its development is influenced by the mental state of the infected person.

Fear accelerates transformation.

Guilt gives it structure.

Faith gives it imagery.

Pain gives it pathways.

Memory gives it names.

The Vale Imprint gives it Hell.

## Stages of Infection

### Stage One: The Whisper

The victim experiences intrusive memories, nightmares, phantom sensations, auditory hallucinations, and emotional instability. They may smell incense where there is none. They may hear bells. They may dream of Europa’s ocean despite never having left Earth.

Physical symptoms include fever, black-gold capillary lines, bleeding gums, light sensitivity, and skin that reacts to spoken prayer or certain sound frequencies.

At this stage, treatment is possible but uncertain.

### Stage Two: The Confession

The body begins to change visibly. Old wounds reopen. Bone growths form beneath the skin. Hands may stiffen into prayer-like positions. Eyes may multiply or cloud over with gold flecks. The victim becomes fixated on guilt, duty, failure, grief, hunger, shame, or judgment.

The person is still themselves, but under pressure. They may beg for help, hide symptoms, become violent, seek priests, seek scientists, or feel drawn toward Bloom zones.

### Stage Three: The Icon

The body reorganizes around an infernal role derived from the victim’s identity and the Vale Imprint. The victim may still speak and remember, but their instincts are altered. Some become predators. Some become guardians. Some become missionaries. Some become stationary shrines of suffering.

Recovery from Stage Three is considered impossible by the Remnant and nearly impossible by the Compact.

### Stage Four: The Choir

The individual self collapses into a larger Host network. The body becomes part of a nest, landscape, living structure, or battlefield organism. It may repeat memories and phrases, but most scholars believe the original person is gone.

The Choir of the Open Wound disagrees.

They believe Stage Four is the beginning of true communion.

---

# Host Forms

The Host’s creatures are not random mutants. They are infernal icons grown from human material. Every design should feel like something trying to be holy and failing horribly.

Common visual features include bone halos, fused prayer-hands, exposed nerves like red scripture, black-gold tissue, thorn growths, false wings, mouth clusters, faces stretched into agony masks, ribs opening like chapel doors, candle-like fat growths, bell organs, wet vestment-like flesh, embedded ship metal, stained-glass eye clusters, and bodies bent into kneeling or cruciform poses.

## Penitent Bastions

Former soldiers, guards, crusaders, or protectors. They become armored with bone plates, fused metal, and shield-like ribs. Many are locked into defensive stances. They cannot flee. They can barely turn away from whatever they are protecting.

They often guard meaningless things: a collapsed doorway, a dead commander, a nursery, an empty supply crate, a flag burned beyond recognition.

## False Saints

Former clergy, prophets, cult leaders, or deeply religious victims. They become radiant grotesques with halos, exposed organs, torn vestments, and voices that mimic prayer. Their blessings infect. Their hymns panic the weak-willed. Their presence organizes lesser Host creatures.

A False Saint should feel like a boss encounter even when it is not one.

## Madonnas of Ash

Former parents, nurses, caretakers, or grieving survivors. They preserve corpses, wounded animals, dolls, machines, and sometimes living victims inside womb-like cavities. They sing. They comfort. They collect.

They are among the most tragic Host forms because their violence resembles love.

## Scholastic Heretics

Former scientists, doctors, engineers, archivists, or teachers. They become many-eyed, tool-fingered examiners covered in equations and prayer scars. They dissect enemies, expose weaknesses, alter nearby Host creatures, and speak with calm academic horror.

Some still ask questions while killing.

## Thuribles

Host organisms that grow from lungs, incense burners, ventilation systems, or infected clergy. They hang from ceilings or archways and release hallucination vapor like incense. Their fumes induce visions of judgment, dead relatives, burning cities, or Europa’s black ocean.

## Choir Larvae

Small creatures grown from children, animals, or incomplete transformations. They repeat phrases from the dead: prayers, distress calls, lullabies, mission logs, jokes, apologies. They are weak alone but devastating in groups.

Not every Choir Larva should attack immediately. Some should force the player to decide whether mercy, caution, or survival comes first.

## Judges

Former magistrates, commanders, bishops, executives, and tyrants. Their bodies become tribunal halls of flesh: multiple mouths, ribcages opening like courtrooms, fingers like gavels, eyes arranged as witness galleries.

They accuse enemies during battle. Their accusations may be based on actual player choices.

## Seraphim Wrecks

Rare Host forms created from pilots, astronauts, crash survivors, or people infected near ship debris. They resemble broken angels made of pressure suits, torn skin, fuel lines, and burning nerve clusters. They fly badly, as if every movement hurts.

They remind the player that the apocalypse fell from the sky.

---

# Important Locations

## Sanctum Aurelian

Capital of the Holy Remnant. A city of bells, walls, floodlights, reliquaries, executions, public kitchens, and hidden laboratories. Its central basilica is built into an ancient orbital defense cannon known as **the Spear of Dawn**. No one knows whether the weapon still works, but everyone behaves as if it might.

Beneath the basilica are the **Mercy Courts**, where suspected infected are tried, examined, tortured, absolved, burned, or quietly transferred to secret research cells.

## Meridian Vault

The Lumen Compact’s strongest city. It lies underground beneath a dead desert, inside old accelerator tunnels and military bunkers. It has clean water, stable power, advanced medicine, and sealed lower levels where experiments failed.

Its greatest secret is **Project Lazarus Bloom**, an attempt to reverse Stage Three infection by reconstructing the original personality from Host-transformed neural tissue.

Some test subjects beg to die.

Some claim they met Father Vale inside the network.

## Veyr’s Gate

The great neutral trade city. Built around the anchor of a destroyed space elevator, it is a vertical maze of markets, slums, militia towers, guild halls, shrines, black clinics, and salvage elevators descending into forbidden depths.

Veyr’s Gate is the best early-game hub. Every faction has a presence there. Every alley can lead to a quest.

## Woundfall

The largest crash scar of _Eschaton’s Mercy_. It is a vast wasteland of fused ship metal, black glass, dormant Host tissue, broken chapels, pressure doors half-buried in earth, and old distress hymns that still play through dead speakers.

At the center lies **the Nave**, the remains of the ship’s chapel and command spine. It may contain the original mission archive, Father Vale’s last transmission, and the truth about the Stilling.

## The Pale Orchard

A dormant Bloom zone where transformed bodies have rooted into the soil like trees. Their faces remain visible in the bark. They whisper when wind passes through them. Some bear pale fruit that can heal radiation sickness, suppress infection, or cause visions.

The Remnant wants to burn the Orchard.

The Compact wants to study it.

The Choir makes pilgrimages there.

Local villages quietly harvest from it because without the fruit, they would starve.

## Low Harrow

A flooded city where people live on rooftops, bridges, and floating platforms. The lower levels are contaminated. The upper levels are alive with markets, boat gangs, rooftop gardens, and old families who know more than they say.

The city survives because of a pact with a dormant Host intelligence beneath the water.

## The Black Reliquary

A walking fortress controlled by the Penitent Engines. It carries armories, chapels, repair bays, relic vaults, prison cells, and hundreds of cybernetic warrior-monks. Its arrival can save a town or doom it, depending on what the Penitents find inside the walls.

## Saint Origen Deep Bore

The original Europa facility. For most of the game it exists as memory, archive footage, hallucination, and myth. In a late-game arc, the player may find a way to recover data from Europa or even return there.

The truth beneath Europa should complicate every faction’s beliefs.

Perhaps the Host was harmless before Vale.

Perhaps it was already waiting for a mind like his.

Perhaps Saint Origen was not humanity’s first contact with it.

# Main Story Campaign

## Player Setup

The player begins as the leader of a small field squad working along the borderlands between Free City territory and old Bloom zones. The squad may be affiliated with a Free City militia, a salvage company, a neutral quarantine office, a mixed expedition funded by several factions, or — as in the current playable vertical slice — the Holy Remnant's **Ashen Censure**, the cult-purgation office whose field agents (cult-breakers) ride writs into silent chapels and stopped towns to put down blasphemous Host-cults. Framed this way, the protagonist has spent years breaking cults for the Church and has privately noted that there are far more of them lately than there used to be; the Ash Chapel on the road to Hallowfen is simply the latest writ, and the one where the larger mystery begins to surface.

Their first mission is ordinary by the standards of the world: investigate a silent settlement, identify whether the outbreak is active, recover survivors if possible, and prevent contaminated material from reaching trade roads.

The settlement is called **Hallowfen**.

Hallowfen should feel human before it becomes horrific. The player finds dinner still on tables, children’s drawings of wall guards, a half-finished wedding dress, repair invoices, local political posters, devotional candles, Compact medicine labels, and smuggler markings near the water pump.

The outbreak appears small at first.

Then the squad finds a chapel basement where the entire town council has fused into a single living tribunal. Its many mouths accuse the player characters by name, including things they have not told each other.

In the basement is a relic: a blackened data core from _Eschaton’s Mercy_, still warm after 130 years.

When touched, it plays a fragment of Father Vale’s voice.

Then a second voice interrupts him.

The second voice belongs to Dr. Elian Sorell, the xenobiologist listed as dead during the Descent.

The message says:

> The Stilling was not divine intervention.
>
> It was a lock.
>
> The lock is failing.
>
> Do not let them find the priest.

This discovery begins the main story.

---

## Act I: Ash Roads

The early game centers on survival, investigation, and faction introduction.

The player must escape Hallowfen, decide what to do with exposed survivors, and choose who receives the recovered data core. Every option has consequences.

Giving it to the Holy Remnant grants protection but risks censorship and execution of witnesses.

Giving it to the Lumen Compact reveals more data but exposes the player to dangerous experiments and political manipulation.

Selling it in Veyr’s Gate brings money and underworld contacts but spreads the secret to multiple factions.

Keeping it makes the player a target.

Act I introduces the world’s unstable balance. The player travels through trade roads, quarantine checkpoints, ruined suburbs, small towns, and the outskirts of Veyr’s Gate. They encounter Remnant inquisitors, Compact field teams, Ash Cartel smugglers, Bloomborn refugees, and Choir missionaries.

Signs of renewed Host activity appear everywhere.

A dormant Choir Tree speaks a sentence recorded only in the Europa mission logs.

A child born in a clean city draws Saint Origen Deep Bore from memory.

A caravan guard transforms after touching a relic that had been safe for decades.

A Penitent Engine patrol abandons its route and marches toward Woundfall, claiming the bells under the earth have started again.

The act ends when the data core is partially decoded. It contains coordinates for three fragments of the original mission archive scattered across Earth during the Descent.

Each fragment is held, hidden, or worshipped by a different faction.

---

## Act II: The Three Witnesses

The player must recover the three archive fragments.

### The First Witness: The Surgeon’s Gospel

The first fragment lies beneath Meridian Vault in the sealed remains of an early Compact laboratory. It contains medical footage from _Eschaton’s Mercy_ showing infected crew members transforming during the return journey.

The key revelation: not every transformation became Hell-like immediately. The first few secondary infections showed unstable, beautiful, alien geometries before Vale’s pattern overwhelmed them. This suggests the Vale Imprint spread like a dominant signal through the organism.

The Compact wants to bury this because it proves their theory but also implies a terrifying possibility: if the Imprint fails, the Host may become something even less predictable.

### The Second Witness: The Confessor’s Key

The second fragment is hidden in Sanctum Aurelian, inside the private vault of the Quiet Cardinals. It contains Father Vale’s pre-mission confessions, including evidence that he believed the Europa mission was spiritually dangerous before launch.

It also reveals that Vale was not merely present for observation. He had been instructed to perform a first-contact rite if alien life was found. The Ecclesiate wanted the encounter framed through human theology from the beginning.

The Holy Remnant will kill to keep this secret.

### The Third Witness: The Bell in the Water

The third fragment lies in Low Harrow, beneath the flooded lower city, inside a section of _Eschaton’s Mercy_ that fell into the old harbor. The local ruling families have concealed it for generations because the dormant Host intelligence beneath the water protects the city in exchange for offerings.

This fragment contains the voice of Dr. Elian Sorell explaining that the Host did not stop because it weakened. It stopped because Sorell used a network of old Ecclesiate systems, machine choirs, and infected tissue to trap the organism inside the Vale Imprint.

The Stilling was not a cure.

It was a cage made from Hell.

---

## Act III: The Politics of Truth

Once the player assembles the archive, every major faction reacts.

The Holy Remnant declares the archive demonic forgery and begins preparing a crusade to seize Woundfall. Pontifex Severian III argues that if the Host is awakening, only unified faith can prevent extinction.

The Lumen Compact demands access to the full data and prepares an expedition to find pre-Vale material. Some Compact leaders believe they can remove the Imprint and restore the Host to its original state. Others believe the Imprint is the only thing keeping the Host recognizable enough to fight.

The Choir of the Open Wound interprets the archive as prophecy. They believe the failing lock means Father Vale is calling the world back into communion. Choir cells begin coordinated attacks, not to destroy cities, but to open gates and expose populations.

The Free Cities panic. Some want to ally with the Remnant. Others seek Compact protection. Others form a defensive league. Refugee crises explode as border towns are abandoned.

The Ash Cartels profit from everyone until the roads become too dangerous even for them.

The Penitent Engines march toward Woundfall without waiting for orders.

During this act, the player’s choices shape which factions survive, which unite, and which become enemies. The player may expose the truth publicly, suppress parts of it, use it to blackmail leaders, or attempt to build a coalition.

The act ends with a major Host awakening at the Pale Orchard.

The Orchard blooms out of season. The rooted dead begin speaking in unison. They do not speak with Father Vale’s voice.

They speak with Dr. Sorell’s.

> The lock is failing because something is pulling from beneath it.
>
> Not the priest.
>
> Not Hell.
>
> Europa.

---

## Act IV: Woundfall

The fourth act centers on the race to reach the Nave at Woundfall, the largest surviving section of _Eschaton’s Mercy_ and the probable center of the planetary lock.

The journey should feel like a dark pilgrimage.

The player crosses dead highways, ash farms, abandoned orbital defense sites, Host battlefields from the Bloom Years, refugee columns, cartel ambushes, Penitent Engine war camps, Remnant crusade lines, Compact forward laboratories, and Choir processions walking barefoot into contamination.

Woundfall itself is a massive explorable region. It is not simply a dungeon. It is a landscape of crashed starship architecture fused with dormant Host growth. Half-buried corridors lead into open wasteland. Old chapels hang upside down. Airlocks open into forests of bone. The ship’s PA system still plays fragments of distress hymns. Gravity plating malfunctions in pockets. Some rooms are preserved exactly as they were during the Descent, as if time inside them stopped.

Inside the Nave, the player discovers the truth of the Stilling.

Dr. Elian Sorell survived the crash, but not as an ordinary human. During the Bloom Years, Sorell became partially integrated with the Host while retaining enough identity to understand its growth. Sorell realized that the Vale Imprint, horrific as it was, gave the Host a limited symbolic structure. As long as the Host remained trapped inside Vale’s Hell, it could be contained, predicted, and slowed.

Without the Imprint, the organism might expand beyond human comprehension.

Sorell used old Ecclesiate satellites, machine choirs, orbital mirrors, confession networks, and infected tissue relays to reinforce the Vale Imprint across Earth. The Stilling occurred when the Host was locked into a recursive pattern: Hell repeating Hell, unable to learn a larger language.

The world survived because the Host was imprisoned inside one priest’s nightmare.

But the lock is failing because something beneath Europa has begun transmitting again.

The organism under the ice was not dead.

It was not fully present on Earth.

And now it is calling its wounded fragment home.

---

## Act V: The Priest and the Ocean

The final act can branch depending on the desired scale of the game.

The player may remain on Earth and decide the fate of the planetary lock, or the story may escalate toward Europa through old launch systems, orbital debris colonies, and abandoned pilgrimage ships.

At the center is the question of Father Vale.

His original transformed body, **the First Icon**, may still exist inside the deepest part of Woundfall, fused into the remains of _Eschaton’s Mercy_. The Remnant claims he was destroyed. The Choir claims he sleeps. The Compact believes only fragments remain. Sorell’s archive suggests Vale’s body is not the lock, but the key that gave the lock its shape.

Finding the First Icon should be one of the game’s most disturbing sequences.

Vale is not a final boss in the simple sense. He is a ruined person, an alien relay, a saint, a victim, a monster, a weapon, and a prison. He may speak lucidly for moments. He may beg the player to kill him. He may insist that Hell is real. He may admit he no longer knows which thoughts are his.

The player must decide what to do with the Host, the Vale Imprint, and the failing lock.

Possible endings include:

### The Fire Ending

The player helps the Holy Remnant activate the Spear of Dawn or another ancient weapon to burn Woundfall and major Host sites. The second Bloom is delayed or prevented, but countless infected, Bloomborn, prisoners, and civilians die. The Remnant emerges as the dominant power and begins forced reunification of Earth.

Humanity survives under bells and fire.

### The Cure Ending

The player helps the Lumen Compact stabilize a treatment derived from Sorell’s work. The cure suppresses early infection and may reverse some Stage Two cases, but it cannot save Stage Three Icons. The Compact gains immense influence. Host activity decreases, but the signal from Europa remains unresolved.

Humanity survives through knowledge and compromise, with many bodies left outside the definition of saved.

### The Open Wound Ending

The player sides with or fails to stop the Choir. The lock breaks. The Host begins a second Bloom, but this time the transformation is stranger. The Vale Imprint weakens. Some people become Hell-forms. Others become alien geometries, impossible ecosystems, or new modes of consciousness. Civilization collapses further, but something new is born.

Humanity does not survive unchanged.

### The Lock Renewed Ending

The player sacrifices the First Icon, Sorell, a companion, or themselves to reinforce the Vale Imprint and maintain the Stilling. The world remains broken but stable. Cities endure. The Host remains trapped in Hell’s shape. Future generations may get another chance.

Humanity survives by preserving a nightmare.

### The Europa Ending

The player reaches or contacts Europa and discovers the source organism beneath the ice. It is not demonic. It is not innocent. It is a vast alien ecology or intelligence that never understood what its Earth fragment became. The player may sever the connection, communicate with it, destroy the relay, or invite a new imprint that could overwrite Vale’s Hell.

Humanity survives only if it dares first contact again, this time without a priest’s fear as the first language.

# Companion Concepts

## Sister Ilyra Voss

A Remnant exorcist from the Mercy Orders. She believes in God, doubts the Pontifex, and has secretly spared infected children. She uses prayer-litanies, fire, and field medicine. Her personal quest forces her to choose between obedience and mercy.

## Dr. Kael Ren

A Lumen Compact xenobiologist. Brilliant, exhausted, and morally compromised. He believes the Vale Imprint theory is correct and fears the Compact will weaponize the truth. His personal quest involves test subjects he abandoned in a sealed lab.

## Mara of the Morrow Chain

A smuggler-guide who knows the roads, cartels, bribes, and lies that keep the Free Cities alive. She claims to care only about payment, but she keeps taking jobs that protect refugees.

## Brother Tarn

A Penitent Engine whose body is mostly machine. He speaks through a damaged vox-grille and records every failure on his armor. His personal quest asks whether duty without tenderness is just another form of surrender.

## Lio

A young Bloomborn tracker with black-gold veins and an instinctive sense for dormant Host tissue. Lio wants citizenship in a city that fears them. Their infection may progress depending on player choices.

## Anselm Quay

A former Choir singer who escaped before full conversion. He knows cult rites, Host hymns, and the location of hidden nests. No one trusts him, including himself.

# Tone and Themes

The world should be horrifying, but not hopeless.

The horror is strongest when there is something worth saving. The player should see rooftop gardens, market festivals, children learning letters under militia watch, priests comforting the dying, scientists repairing water systems, Bloomborn poets hiding in slums, smugglers risking their lives for medicine, soldiers joking before entering quarantine, and monsters leaving flowers at houses they half remember.

This is not a world where everyone is evil.

It is a world where every form of goodness is under pressure.

## Fear Made Flesh

The Host’s transformations are not random. They are bodies turned into accusations. The apocalypse is psychological, religious, and biological at once.

## Faith After Catastrophe

Religion is not simply wrong. It comforts, organizes, heals, and protects. It also censors, burns, lies, and controls. The central question is not whether faith is foolish, but what happens when faith encounters something that can wear faith’s nightmares as flesh.

## Science Without Innocence

Science can explain the Host better than doctrine can, but explanation does not guarantee mercy. The Compact’s tragedy is that truth can become another altar.

## Survival Versus Humanity

Cities survive by making cruel rules. The player must constantly decide when protection becomes oppression and when mercy becomes recklessness.

## The Body as Battlefield

In this setting, politics, trauma, religion, alien biology, and identity all fight over the human body. The body is not stable. It can be judged, rewritten, weaponized, worshipped, or saved.

# Style Guide for Writing This World

Use religious language, but keep it grounded in material reality. A cathedral may also be a reactor. A relic may also be contaminated metal. A prayer may also be a breathing exercise that lowers infection risk. A miracle may be misunderstood technology. A scientific breakthrough may look like blasphemy.

Avoid making the Host look like generic red flesh. Its visual identity should be infernal, sacred, and alien at once: black-gold filaments, bone halos, wet vestment-like tissue, nerve-light, thorned growth, false wings, fused hands, bell organs, stained-glass eyes, and ship-metal relics embedded in bodies.

Do not make every religious character cruel. Do not make every scientist wise. Do not make every mutant innocent. Do not make every monster mindless.

The best stories in this world should leave the player asking not only, “What is the right choice?” but also, “Who gets to decide what counts as human?”

## Public Knowledge vs. Hidden Truth (hard writing rule)

This is a binding rule for all **player-facing** text — in-game dialogue, notes, item descriptions, the opening writ, barks, UI.

The general population, and the overwhelming majority of the Holy Remnant itself, **does not know the Host is alien.** They believe what the state teaches: that the Descent opened a wound in creation, that **Hell itself is invading the world**, and that the Remnant wages **God's war** against the damned. The hyper-religious authoritarian state enforces this narrative on purpose, because "we are holding back Hell, so obey us" is the most powerful instrument it has for controlling a terrified population. Doubt is heresy; heresy burns.

The real truth — that the Host is an **alien organism** from beneath Europa's ice, shaped into Hell's image by **Father Vale's** mind (the Vale Imprint), and that the Stilling is a **failing containment lock** rather than a divine mercy — is known only to a select few: the Quiet Cardinals, senior Lumen Compact leadership, the Choir's inner circle, and figures like Sorell. To say aloud that Vale shaped the Host, or that it is biology rather than damnation, is heresy punishable by fire.

Therefore, player-facing text must **never casually reveal** alien origin, Europa, first contact, the starship, or the Vale Imprint by name. Describe the horror the way a believer or a weary Censure agent would: Hell, damnation, false saints, the rot, the Host. The deeper truth is reached only by **mystery and investigation** — a child drawing a cold ringed moon no one taught them, a Compact note that treats demons like lab samples, a cult that calls damnation "confession," a lock that is quietly failing. Let players *feel* the official story cracking long before anyone hands them the answer. The "fell from the sky / a cold moon" imagery is permitted precisely because, to an in-world mind, it is inexplicable and frightening, not proof of anything.

# Opening Crawl

> NOTE: This crawl states the hidden truth plainly. It is the THEMATIC / late-reveal framing and dev canon — it is **not** what the protagonist or the public knows at the start of the game. The in-game opening (see the Ash Chapel "Field Writ of the Ashen Censure") deliberately tells only the doctrinal version. Hold this crawl for a late reveal, not the cold open.

Humanity did not abandon faith when it reached the stars.

It armored faith in steel, taught it mathematics, crowned it with satellites, and sent it drilling beneath the ice of Europa.

There, in an ocean without sunrise, we found life.

The first man it touched was a priest.

The organism learned him before it learned us.

It learned sin.

It learned angels.

It learned punishment.

It learned Hell.

Then it came home.

For ten years, Earth bloomed.

Cities became wounds. Armies became processions. Saints screamed from towers of bone and wire.

Then the Bloom stopped.

No one knows why.

A century later, the walls still stand. The bells still ring. The laboratories still burn through the night. The faithful blame the Devil. The scientists blame the mission. The hungry blame whoever has bread.

But beneath the old crash sites, something has begun to sing again.

And this time, it knows us better.

# Sample In-World Texts

## Fragment from Father Marius Vale’s Private Confession

I have argued in council that alien life, if discovered, must be greeted with humility. I have written that creation is larger than fear. I have said these things in public and believed myself brave.

But in sleep I see an ocean under ice, and beneath the ocean a door, and behind the door a choir with no mouths.

I fear we do not go to meet a neighbor.

I fear we go to give our oldest nightmare a body.

## Lumen Compact Field Note

The subject’s transformation does not correspond to local belief structures. Subject was raised in a secular Free City with minimal doctrinal exposure. Despite this, morphological development included halo formation, rib-wing extension, prayer-hand fusion, and vocal repetition of Ecclesiate penitential phrases.

Conclusion: infernal morphology is not culturally acquired at point of infection. It is inherited through the organism itself.

The organism is not responding to the subject’s religion.

It is imposing Vale’s.

## Sermon of Pontifex Severian III

They will tell you the Devil is a microbe.

They will tell you Hell is chemistry.

They will tell you sin is a pattern written by frightened cells.

Listen carefully when they speak this way. Hear the old pride returning. Hear Europa drilling downward again.

A thing that wears the shape of damnation has entered the world. Whether it came by moon or gate or ocean is dust beside the truth of its works.

Fire knows what argument forgets.

# Core Design Promise

This setting is about a future where humanity’s first contact with alien life was poisoned by fear, doctrine, guilt, and the need to control meaning.

The alien organism did not arrive as Hell.

It became Hell because the first human it truly knew believed in Hell completely.

Now every city, faction, companion, monster, and quest should orbit that wound.

The player is not simply fighting an infection.

They are fighting a nightmare that became hereditary.
