# Originality Audit

This document answers a practical story question: does the campaign feel recycled from other CRPGs, and how do we keep it from drifting into familiar genre paste?

## Verdict

The story uses familiar CRPG materials, but the current version is not recycled if we protect the causal chain.

Familiar materials:

- Faction politics.
- A cult that is more than random raiders.
- Forbidden archives and staged revelations.
- A late-game truth that changes the public history.
- A boss that appears religious or supernatural.
- Endings tied to faction commitments.

Those are genre tools. They become stale only if they sit on the surface. This story has a specific engine underneath them:

1. Humanity finds alien life under Europa.
2. The first human mind it deeply learns is Father Marius Vale.
3. Vale's fear of Hell becomes inherited biological grammar.
4. Seneca saves humanity by turning that grammar into a lock.
5. The Choir mistakes the lock's failure for revelation.
6. The Devil manifestation is Host-made doctrine, not proof that doctrine was right.
7. The ending asks whether one man's eternal torture can remain the price of survival.

That chain is the original spine. If a quest, faction, or boss does not connect back to it through human need, political pressure, bodily horror, or belief under stress, it risks becoming generic.

## Where It Could Feel Recycled

The campaign can accidentally resemble stock CRPG structure in five places.

### Faction Hub Drift

Risk: Act II becomes a standard tour of church faction, science faction, rebel faction, cult faction.

Guardrail: each faction must be right about one survival need and wrong about one moral cost. The Remnant keeps roads and hospitals alive, but burns truth. The Compact can cure more than anyone else, but turns victims into evidence. Free Cities protect ordinary life, but their compromises can become quiet cruelty. The Choir shelters outcasts, then asks them to pay with their bodies.

### Archive Hunt Drift

Risk: the Three Witnesses become three lore keys collected before the real game resumes.

Guardrail: each Witness changes what the player believes the campaign is about.

- The Confessor's Key: first contact was a priest, not a neutral scientific accident.
- The Surgeon's Gospel: early Host forms were not only Hell-shaped, so the Vale Imprint is learned and inherited.
- The Bell in the Water: Seneca made the Stilling lock, calcified into it, and cannot save it alone.

The player should need investigation, local politics, witness testimony, companion knowledge, and field consequences to find them. Seneca gives puzzle clues, not map pins.

### Evil Cult Drift

Risk: the Choir becomes a predictable murder cult with red symbols and a demon boss.

Guardrail: Act III must show why people join. Hospices, rejected families, Bloomborn shelter, grief, hunger, shame, and fear should all be visible before the worst rites are revealed. Thecla is dangerous because she offers belonging and turns that belonging into consent for horror.

The vats and total immersion rite should be awful, but not random. The Choir is trying to make its theology physically true.

### Demon Boss Drift

Risk: the Devil manifestation reads as a conventional fantasy demon climax.

Guardrail: in the moment, everyone should feel doctrine become real. Afterward, the truth must be worse and stranger: the Host made a supreme Adversary Icon from harvested Host matter, the victim's fear, Remnant doctrine, and the failing Vale Imprint lock.

This is not "the Devil is real." It is "a living organism learned how to make people see the Devil they already feared."

### Final Source Drift

Risk: Act IV becomes a normal final dungeon where the player kills the source monster.

Guardrail: the final chamber is a moral crime scene. Seneca is fused into the lock and saved the world by preserving Vale's Hell. Vale is victim, relay, monster, witness, and torturer. The player is not deciding whether evil dies. The player is deciding what price survival is allowed to charge.

## Act Shape

The current four-act shape helps the story feel specific.

### Act I: Narrow

Ash Road and Hallowfen stay local. The player should care about a settlement, a wall, checkpoint bells, missing families, compromised guards, and the first proof that this new cult is coordinated. The chapel and road encounters must not reveal Seneca, Vale, Europa, or the full Host truth too early.

### Act II: Wide

This is the biggest act. It explores towns, faction courts, water politics, road economies, Bloomborn citizenship, Compact medicine, Remnant dissent, companion arcs, and local survival. The Witnesses are the spine, not the whole body.

### Act III: Narrow

The focus tightens around the Choir's lair and way of life. The Pontifex abduction turns investigation into crisis. The moon sigil means something to the player because of prior investigation, not because an NPC explains it immediately.

### Act IV: Abyss

Woundfall is not only the crash site. It is the place where the world-saving lie becomes a person in a wall and a priest who cannot escape his own fear. Faction endings still matter, but the emotional center is Vale and Seneca.

## What Makes This Setting Itself

Keep these elements central:

- The Host is not morally evil by default. It reproduces a human fear pattern.
- The Vale Imprint is not flavor. It explains enemy forms, faction theology, public lies, and endgame choice.
- Faith is not only oppression. It comforts and organizes people under impossible pressure.
- Science is not innocent. Explanation can become ownership.
- The Choir is not random madness. It is a refuge that becomes a body-theology machine.
- The Devil manifestation is a false confirmation that becomes politically explosive before it is understood.
- Seneca is neither savior nor villain. Seneca committed an unforgivable act that may still be necessary.
- Vale is not a simple source monster. He is the accidental author of the Host's Earth language and the person most harmed by the lock.

## Drift Tests

Before adding a major quest, boss, or faction branch, ask:

1. What ordinary human need does this touch?
2. What does the player learn through action, not a lore dump?
3. Which public belief does this scene appear to confirm?
4. What hidden truth later complicates that belief?
5. How does the Vale Imprint matter here?
6. What would make this choice painful even if the player knows the truth?

If the answer is only "this advances the plot," the scene is too generic.

## One-Paragraph Pitch

This is a religious science-horror CRPG about a ruined Earth where alien life did not arrive as a demon, but learned Hell from the first priest it touched. Humanity survived because one scientist trapped the organism inside that priest's fear, freezing the apocalypse into a lock that is now failing. The player follows a damaged warning, uncovers three Witnesses, confronts a cult trying to manufacture Satan from living Host matter, then reaches the final chamber where the savior who made the lock and the priest tortured by it both demand release.
