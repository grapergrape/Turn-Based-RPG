# World

## Overview

The canonical setting is defined by `docs/lore/the_host_story_bible.md`. This file is the game-facing world guide: what the player can understand, where the campaign travels, which factions shape play, and what tone every quest should preserve.

The game takes place roughly 130 years after **the Descent**, when the infected research-pilgrimage vessel _Eschaton's Mercy_ broke apart over Earth and seeded the world with the Europa organism now called **The Host**. Humanity survived the first apocalypse because the Bloom stopped. That halt is called **the Stilling**.

The public does not know the truth. The Holy Remnant calls the Stilling divine mercy. The Lumen Compact calls it a biological limit. The Choir of the Open Wound calls it a pause before communion. The campaign reveals that the Stilling was an artificial lock created by Dr. Elian Sorell to trap The Host inside **the Vale Imprint**, the inherited psychic-biological pattern left by Father Marius Vale.

The current era is the **Age of Ashen Cities**. Earth is broken but alive. Cities still farm, trade, pray, repair walls, hold courts, raise children, and make ugly compromises. The old stability is failing: dormant Host tissue pulses again, Choir Trees speak new phrases, minor exposures are becoming more severe, and low-frequency signals are moving beneath old crash sites.

## Canon Rules

- The alien organism is called **The Host** in common speech.
- The original Europa organism may be broader than The Host as Earth knows it.
- Every Earthborn Host strain descends from the sample that infected Father Marius Vale.
- The **Vale Imprint** is why Host forms become religious body horror instead of random alien mutations.
- The Host is not proven to be morally evil, but it reproduces Vale's inherited vision of damnation.
- The Stilling was not a cure. It was containment.
- The Stilling both calcified the Bloom-era Host and left an ongoing lock. Calcified hosts are frozen mid-change with the lights on, faintly aware, not dead. Infection after the Stilling still transforms new victims, but it can no longer chain into a Bloom unless deliberately fed. As the lock fails, calcified hosts can thaw and fresh infection can begin to catch again.
- The new crisis is caused by the lock failing as something beneath Europa calls the Earth fragment home.
- The Choir of the Open Wound is a recently emerged, poorly understood Host-cult, distinct from the common older Host-cults the Censure routinely burns. Field agents meet it as an unknown and investigate it; its rapid, coordinated spread is itself a symptom of the failing lock.

## Historical Spine

### The Solar Ecclesiate

Before the fall, humanity was ruled by the Solar Ecclesiate, a technologically advanced religious state that fused church, government, military, science, and infrastructure. It consecrated science rather than rejecting it. Laboratories were chapels, AIs were machine choirs, and space exploration was framed as sacred expansion.

The Ecclesiate sent scientists, soldiers, engineers, and Father Marius Vale to Europa because alien life would have been both a scientific discovery and a theological crisis.

### Saint Origen Deep Bore

Saint Origen Deep Bore was the Europan drilling monastery where humanity first touched the organism beneath the ice. The official mission was the Jovian Communion Initiative. Private files show the Ecclesiate expected life and wanted the discovery controlled.

Father Vale entered the containment chamber and blessed the sample. The organism breached containment, entered his body, and interpreted him.

### The First Infection

The organism did not simply consume Vale. It learned his doctrine, fear, guilt, memories, images of Hell, and need for divine meaning. Vale's transformation gave the organism a human symbolic grammar. This became the Vale Imprint.

The Host does not reveal the true self of victims. It condemns victims through Vale's idea of Hell.

### The Descent

The infected vessel _Eschaton's Mercy_ returned toward Earth carrying survivors, samples, archives, contaminated equipment, and Host growth. Earth's defense grid failed to destroy it. The ship broke apart during atmospheric entry, scattering infected debris across the planet. Hallowfen became the first crash scar most survivors later named as the Earthside origin of the Host.

This event is called the Descent.

### The Bloom Years

For roughly ten years, The Host expanded through crash sites, hospitals, refugee routes, military perimeters, graveyards, churches, prisons, and battlefields. Bloom zones were not just infected land. They were landscapes rewritten by the Vale Imprint.

The Solar Ecclesiate collapsed. Off-world colonies abandoned Earth. The first apocalypse seemed unstoppable.

### The Stilling

The Bloom stopped. Dormant flesh hardened, spores weakened, rivers cleared, and active growth became dangerous but geographically limited. The hosts caught in the freeze did not die; they calcified mid-transformation, still faintly aware, which is why dormant Host sites are treated as sleeping rather than spent. Infection after the Stilling still opens new victims, but it no longer catches and spreads on its own the way it did in the Bloom. The Stilling allowed humanity to survive.

The hidden truth is that Dr. Elian Sorell used old Ecclesiate systems, machine choirs, orbital mirrors, confession networks, and infected tissue relays to trap The Host in a recursive version of Vale's nightmare. Humanity survived because The Host was imprisoned inside one priest's Hell.

### The Age of Ashen Cities

The game begins in the broken but living world after the Stilling. No global government remains. Surviving power belongs to fortified cities, vaults, religious courts, militias, cults, cartels, mobile war-monasteries, and communities too small to appear on maps.

The central present-tense conflict is not "a new infection starts." It is that every faction built its politics around a containment system that is now failing.

## Regions

### Hallowfen

The opening settlement and first major Act I crisis. Hallowfen sits near the borderlands between Free City territory and old Bloom zones, but it is older than the current border. A section of _Eschaton's Mercy_ fell there during the Descent, and the Holy Remnant built a wall around the crash scar. The Remnant calls the site holy ground. Smugglers, gamblers, bandits, failed pilgrims, relic thieves, black-market doctors, and glory hunters call it a way to get rich or die known.

The bell towers are on the wall checkpoints, not in the town itself. The opening problem is that those checkpoint towers stop answering. The first route in is the canon route: a checkpoint still pretending to be Remnant-held, already captured by a Choir gang. The alternate route is a smuggler tunnel under the wall.

Hallowfen must feel human before it becomes horrific, but its human life is a lawless settlement under siege: gambling debts, illegal pumps, market-table beds, cheap relic stalls, smuggler marks, Compact medicine labels, wall graffiti, barricades, and people who entered for fortune but stayed because the wall cuts both ways.

Act I asks what happens to Hallowfen. The player can help its people break the siege, steer attackers away long enough for partial evacuation, or abandon the town. If abandoned, later reports reveal that the Choir crucified the whole town upside down to decorate its new temple-capital at the heart of the holy land.

The discovery beneath Hallowfen still leads into the wider campaign, but not as the first Act I reveal. After Hallowfen's fate is settled, the blackened _Eschaton's Mercy_ data core can carry Sorell's warning that the Stilling was a lock, the lock is failing, and no one must find the priest.

### Ash Roads

The early-game travel region between Hallowfen's wall, quarantine chapels, small Free City settlements, abandoned suburbs, road shrines, salvage stops, and the outskirts of Veyr's Gate. The Ash Roads introduce practical survival, faction checkpoints, caravan politics, and the first signs that old Host sites are waking.

The current playable slice, **Ash Chapel Breach**, fits here as a roadside quarantine chapel encounter. It is not the whole Hallowfen sequence, but it should support the same mood: old faith, failed quarantine, cartel scavenging, captured bells, and active Host tissue.

### Veyr's Gate

The best early-game hub. Veyr's Gate is a vertical trade city built around the anchor ruins of a destroyed space elevator. Every faction has spies there. Water rights, gate access, forged papers, relic weapons, suppressants, forbidden books, and black clinics drive its politics.

Veyr's Gate should be noisy, crowded, corrupt, useful, and alive. It is the place where the player's secret can be sold, protected, leaked, or weaponized.

### Sanctum Aurelian

Capital of the Holy Remnant. Sanctum Aurelian is built around an old planetary defense complex and the basilica-weapon called **the Spear of Dawn**. It offers order, medicine, food, law, education, and spiritual meaning. It also burns suspected carriers, censors Europa records, and hides secret priest-surgeon laboratories beneath its courts.

The Confessor's Key archive fragment is hidden here in the private vaults of the Quiet Cardinals.

### Meridian Vault

The Lumen Compact's strongest settlement, built underground inside accelerator tunnels and hardened military bunkers. It has clean water, power, surgical theaters, machine libraries, schools, and sealed lower levels where experiments failed.

Meridian Vault is where the player sees the Compact's contradiction most clearly: truth-seeking, real medicine, and real cruelty. The Surgeon's Gospel archive fragment lies below it.

### Low Harrow

A flooded city where people live on rooftops, bridges, floating markets, and upper floors while the contaminated lower districts glow under black water. Its ruling families conceal a generational pact with a dormant Host intelligence below.

The Bell in the Water archive fragment lies in a submerged section of _Eschaton's Mercy_ beneath the city.

### The Pale Orchard

A dormant Bloom zone where transformed bodies have rooted into the soil like trees. The Orchard can heal, poison, feed, or prophesy depending on who harvests it and why. The Remnant wants to burn it. The Compact wants to study it. The Choir makes pilgrimages there. Local villages quietly rely on it.

The Act III awakening at the Pale Orchard proves that the failing lock is being pulled from Europa, not simply by Vale or Sorell.

### Woundfall

The largest crash scar of _Eschaton's Mercy_ and the Act IV pilgrimage region. Woundfall is not just a dungeon. It is a wasteland of ship metal, dormant Host growth, upside-down chapels, broken pressure doors, black glass, ancient distress hymns, and rooms preserved as if time stopped.

At the center lies **the Nave**, the remains of the ship's chapel and command spine. The Nave is the probable center of the planetary lock and the place where the player learns the truth about Sorell.

### The Black Reliquary

A walking fortress controlled by the Penitent Engines. It carries armories, chapels, repair bays, relic vaults, prison cells, and cybernetic warrior-monks. Its arrival can save a settlement or doom it, depending on what it finds behind the walls.

### Saint Origen Deep Bore and Europa

Saint Origen is the original first-contact site beneath Europa's ice. For most of the campaign it appears through archive footage, hallucination, myth, recovered data, and faction doctrine. In the largest version of the endgame, the player may contact or reach Europa and confront the source organism directly.

## Factions

### Holy Remnant

The largest surviving religious power. It preserves roads, granaries, hospitals, schools, laws, and decontamination rites. It also burns carriers, censors records, kidnaps scientists, and treats the Vale Imprint truth as a threat to its authority.

Its central fear is not simply that the Host exists. Its central fear is that Hell's shape came from inside the Church.

### Lumen Compact

A coalition of scientists, physicians, engineers, archivists, and former Ecclesiate scholars. It is most likely to tell the player the truth and most likely to treat the player as expendable if truth requires it.

The Compact preserves knowledge and medicine. It also experiments on prisoners, hides failed outbreaks, and sometimes confuses explanation with absolution.

### Free Cities

Independent settlements that survive by trade, agriculture, salvage, diplomacy, militia defense, and compromise. They are the emotional center of the world because ordinary life is most visible there.

The Free Cities are not pure democrats or generic rebels. They are practical communities asking how much cruelty is acceptable if it keeps the walls standing.

### Choir of the Open Wound

A Host-worshipping movement that calls infection confession and Host creatures icons. It shelters outcasts, rejected Bloomborn, plague victims, criminals, and refugees, then asks them to pay with their bodies. Unlike the common road-cults the Censure has always burned, the Choir is a recent arrival: it surfaced only in the last few years, across long-quiet regions, already speaking one coherent doctrine, and almost no one who fights it yet knows its name. Its sudden, coordinated rise is a symptom of the failing lock, though it is not yet read as one.

The Choir wants to restart the Bloom, or in its own language, finish the hymn.

### Penitent Engines

Cybernetic warrior-monks descended from old Ecclesiate military orders. They believe steel remembers duty better than blood. Some serve the Remnant, some reject central authority, and some wander as monster hunters, relic guards, or pilgrims of violence.

They fight the Host because they hate the body's capacity to betray the will, but their mutilation is its own tragedy.

### Ash Cartels

Smuggler leagues, raider armies, mercenary clans, salvage guilds, and armed trade networks. They move water, medicine, weapons, relics, stolen people, suppressants, corpses, secrets, and lies.

Some cartels are monsters. Some keep trade routes alive. Many are both.

### Bloomborn

Humans altered by The Host who did not become fully monstrous. Some survived early-stage infection. Some were born to exposed parents. Some were rescued from dormant Bloom zones. They may have black-gold veins, echoing voices, extra pupils, scripture-like scars, or more visible changes while retaining personhood.

Every faction argues over whether they are citizens, patients, weapons, miracles, research material, or threats.

## Tone and Themes

The world should be horrific but not hopeless. Horror works because there is something worth saving.

Use these themes as checks on plot work:

- **Fear made flesh:** Host forms are bodies turned into accusations.
- **Faith after catastrophe:** Religion comforts, organizes, heals, censors, burns, and lies.
- **Science without innocence:** The Compact explains the Host better than doctrine does, but explanation does not guarantee mercy.
- **Survival versus humanity:** Cities survive by making cruel rules. The player decides when protection becomes oppression and when mercy becomes recklessness.
- **The body as battlefield:** Politics, trauma, alien biology, religion, and identity all fight over the human body.

Do not write the world as "religion bad, science good" or "mutation equals purity." Every major faction should be able to save someone, ruin someone, tell one truth, and hide one sin.
