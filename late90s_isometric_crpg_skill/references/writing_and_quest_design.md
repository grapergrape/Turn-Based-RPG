# Writing and Quest Design Reference

## Tone target

The writing should feel:

- dry
- grim
- practical
- morally ambiguous
- slightly absurd
- suspicious of authority
- shaped by scarcity
- grounded in place
- consequence-driven

Avoid:

- heroic fantasy prophecy
- constant snark
- clean good-versus-evil morality
- endless lore dumps
- modern sitcom quips
- overly self-aware memes
- exposition with no player choice

## Dialogue philosophy

NPCs should speak from their needs, fears, beliefs, and leverage.

A good dialogue scene should reveal at least one of:

- what the NPC wants
- what the NPC fears
- what the NPC is hiding
- what faction pressure exists
- what the player can exploit
- what happened in the location
- how the world works

## Dialogue options

Use branching options with different approaches:

- honest question
- lie
- threat
- bribe
- technical observation
- medical observation
- religious/cultural reference
- faction leverage
- barter
- leave
- attack where appropriate

Example format:

```text
1. “Who sealed the chapel?”
2. [Speech] “The Wardens sent me. Open the gate before they count keys.”
3. [Medicine] “That cough is wet. You have maybe two days unless I look at it.”
4. [Repair] “Your lock is wired backward. I can fix it, or I can make it fail loudly.”
5. “Forget it.”
```

## Skill checks

Skill checks should reveal new ways to act, not merely give bonus loot.

Good skill check uses:

- unlock a peaceful path
- detect a lie
- read environmental evidence
- repair a machine
- treat a wound
- bypass combat
- open a sealed route
- expose faction deception
- gain reputation
- create a future consequence

## Quest structure

Every major quest needs:

- premise
- location
- factions/NPCs
- visible conflict
- hidden truth
- at least three solution paths
- at least one non-combat path
- at least one skill-based path
- failure state
- world-state consequence
- journal updates
- inspection text
- reward tradeoff

## Environmental writing

Inspection text should be short, sharp, and atmospheric.

Good object descriptions:

```text
The wax has melted into the cracks like it was trying to escape.
The lock is newer than the door.
Someone scratched names into the bench. Most are crossed out.
The blood is old. The flies are not.
A quarantine notice. The ink has faded, but the threats have not.
```

Avoid long encyclopedia entries unless the player explicitly opens a document.

## Faction writing

Factions need material interests.

Bad faction design:

- evil cult because evil
- raiders because raiders
- guards because guards
- church because religion

Better faction design:

- controls water
- controls roads
- controls medicine
- controls burial rights
- controls electricity
- controls old documents
- controls weapons
- controls food distribution
- controls access to safe tunnels

## Consequences

Consequences should affect later play.

Examples:

- prices change
- NPC leaves or dies
- settlement locks a gate
- faction patrols increase
- rumor spreads
- companion approves/disapproves
- a later ambush happens
- a route opens
- a doctor refuses service
- a grave appears
- an epilogue slide changes

## Journal style

Keep journal text concise and diegetic.

Example:

```text
Ash Chapel Breach

The player believes the sealed lockbox near the altar contains a road seal. The Wardens claim the chapel is condemned, but their lock is newer than the quarantine notice.

Leads:
- Inspect the altar lockbox.
- Ask the red-masked guard about the key.
- Find another way into the cellar.
- Learn why the chapel was sealed again.
```

## Dark humor

Use dark humor sparingly.

It should come from:

- bureaucracy surviving the apocalypse
- people treating horrifying things as normal
- petty local politics
- old signs that no longer fit reality
- practical understatement

Example:

```text
NOTICE: Unlicensed burials will be fined. Repeat offenders will be exhumed.
```

## Voice consistency

Characters should not all speak the same way.

Differences may come from:

- education
- region
- faction training
- religious language
- old-world jargon
- trauma
- greed
- profession
- age
- physical illness
